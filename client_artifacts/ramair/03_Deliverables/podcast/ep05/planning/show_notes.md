**Theme:** Negative Air vs. Flex Duct: What the New Report Really Shows
**Core promise:** Why negative-air duct cleaning is mechanically risky for flexible duct
systems, what the new lab findings reveal, and why positive air duct cleaning is safer.
**Guests:** David Hart (report author) · John Miles (co-researcher, adhesive/materials expert)

## Section topics (intended)
1. Flex duct is designed for positive pressure, not negative (high shear / low peel strength;
   tolerates only 1/2"–1" WC negative pressure; neg-air equipment exceeds this).
2. Negative air causes specific failure modes (adhesive seam peeling/delamination, liner
   collapse around the wire coil, perforations, airflow deformation).
3. Mechanical agitation dramatically increases damage (Element Materials Technology lab tests;
   neg pressure + air-whip agitation = holes + adhesive peel in 12" and 19" ducts).
4. Much damage is hidden / undetectable by standard visual inspection (internal to the duct
   wall; outer jacket masks it; post-clean visual checks insufficient).
5. Negative air creates pathways for moisture and mold (compromised seams draw humid attic/
   crawlspace air -> condensation, microbial growth, recurring contamination).
6. A "safe" negative-air protocol is impractical (3 safeguards: pre-clean inspection, pressure-
   tolerance verification, real-time multi-point monitoring; rarely feasible in the field).
7. Positive pressure cleaning is mechanically compatible (works with the duct's design,
   stays in the strong shear zone, no inward collapse, no extensive ID/monitoring).
8. Current industry standards fall short (don't distinguish rigid vs flexible duct; widespread
   neg-air use on flex duct -> undetected failures + compromised IAQ).
